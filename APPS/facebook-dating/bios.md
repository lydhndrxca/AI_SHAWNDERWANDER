# Facebook Dating — Profile (v3)

A complete Facebook Dating profile package: **one substantive bio** + **3 chosen prompts × 10 answer options each** (30 prompt answers in total).

## What changed from v2

- **One bio, not five.** FB Dating gives you a single 500-char paragraph as your headline — the bio is the most expensive real estate on the profile. Spending it on a one-line gimmick wastes the slot. v3 uses the full space.
- **Mined the interview deeply.** Pulled the lines that aren't on every profile — your career path (pilot → animation → games), the "Shoots and Ladders" conversation metaphor, the Uncle-Sean-in-airports moment, "I'd rather make than watch" as a thesis. Skipped the over-used hobby list.
- **No stat-sheet.** Age, location, marital/kid status, height — those are filled in by FB Dating's structured profile fields. The bio is for voice.
- **Added the prompts work.** FB Dating is bio + 3 prompts. The bio sets the tone; the prompts give you 3 more shots at conversation hooks.

# THE BIO

```
My career went pilot, then 3D animation, then video games — curiosity is a polite word for it. What's underneath the path is just this: I'd rather make than watch. Songs with my band, miniature TV sets, pies on Sundays for whoever's around. The kind of close-with-his-family that gets called Uncle in airports. Conversations should work like Shoots and Ladders — go where they go — and the best jokes come from telling the truth. No rush, but I know what I'm building toward.
```

**~485 / 500 chars.**

### What this bio does (and why each line earns its space)

| Line | What it does |
|------|--------------|
| *"My career went pilot, then 3D animation, then video games — curiosity is a polite word for it."* | Most distinctive single sentence available to you. The pilot → animation → games pivot is rare and earns attention without trying. "Curiosity is a polite word for it" is a self-aware close that sets the voice register for the whole bio. |
| *"What's underneath the path is just this: I'd rather make than watch."* | A thesis. Ties the career to the hobbies to the sensibility. Gives the reader a mental hook to remember you by — most bios don't have one. |
| *"Songs with my band, miniature TV sets, pies on Sundays for whoever's around."* | Three concrete, specific images that imply a whole lifestyle without listing hobbies as bullet points. The "for whoever's around" is the warmth tell — it implies hosting, generosity, social life. |
| *"The kind of close-with-his-family that gets called Uncle in airports."* | Uses your real moment from the interview (the 200-kid school assembly) compressed into one image. Family-close signal without saying "family-oriented." Distinctive. |
| *"Conversations should work like Shoots and Ladders — go where they go — and the best jokes come from telling the truth."* | Two of your own ideas from the interview, lightly polished. Signals: depth-conversationalist + comedic sensibility + has actual taste. |
| *"No rush, but I know what I'm building toward."* | Closes the relationship-readiness signal without using a single dating-cliché word. "No rush" + "I know what I'm building toward" is the exact register of a 41-year-old who isn't desperate but isn't passive either. |

### What it intentionally doesn't include

- Your age (handled by profile field)
- Your location (handled by profile field)
- "Never married, no kids" (handled by relationship-goal + family-plan fields)
- Your height (handled by profile field)
- A list of hobbies (replaced by the three concrete images)
- A CTA (replaced by the closing sentence — the reader can react to it without being told to)
- Adjectives describing yourself (replaced by behaviors)

# THE 3 PROMPTS — chosen and why

Facebook Dating shows 3 prompts alongside the bio. The optimal trio gives you **three different surfaces of personality**: a scene, a filter, and a depth signal.

| Prompt | What it surfaces | Why for you specifically |
|--------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **My idea of a perfect day is…** | A scene the reader places themselves in | Highest-leverage compatibility prompt per FB Dating research; you have rich material here (slow mornings, lake walks, cooking, cabin) |
| **Unpopular opinion…** | A filter — voice, humor, willingness to hold a position | You literally listed 4 strong unpopular opinions in the interview. Wasting them is the actual unpopular opinion |
| **My favorite topic of conversation is…** | A depth signal — what you're like in a long conversation | Your "Shoots and Ladders" framing + Stradivarius/Little Ice Age fact + history/psychology/universe interests = the most undersold part of you |

## PROMPT 1 — "My idea of a perfect day is…"

10 answer options. Each is 1–3 sentences. Different angles so you can pick what feels closest.

### 1.1 — Slow start (the "default beautiful" answer)

```
Slow morning, an absurd amount of coffee, a walk before the city's awake. Back to whatever I'm building that week — usually a song, a model, or a pie. Dinner somewhere with low lights and good bread. Reading on the couch with the windows open.
```

### 1.2 — Maker + dinner + bed-by-11 (the most you)

```
Half of it's in the kitchen — bread, coffee, a pie if it's Sunday. The other half is at the whiteboard wall in my apartment, working out an idea that's been bothering me all week. Dinner with friends I'd let into my house at midnight. Bed by 11 because I'm not 25.
```

### 1.3 — Up north (regional, masculine, specific)

```
Wake up at the cabin up north. Jump into water cold enough to wake up the spine. Bad pancakes, good coffee. Spend the afternoon clearing brush with my dad and hating it in a way that's deeply satisfying. Drive home with the windows down.
```

### 1.4 — Curating it for someone else (your stated love language)

```
I plan it for someone else. Pick three places they don't know exist, build a route, bring snacks. Watch them slowly figure out we're going to a record store, a hidden lake, and a restaurant I read about three years ago and saved for the right occasion.
```

### 1.5 — Pilot twist (uses your unused-best material)

```
I'd fly somewhere small at sunrise — I used to do this, I miss it. Land somewhere with one good diner. Drive back. Cook for whoever's around. End the day with a guitar I'm not great at and a conversation that doesn't need to land anywhere.
```

### 1.6 — The four corners (a quiet thesis)

```
Coffee on the porch. A long walk somewhere I haven't been. A nap I didn't plan for. Dinner with people I love. A pickleball game I lose. One conversation that goes too late. The day's good if I touched all four corners of myself.
```

### 1.7 — Travel-day

```
On the road. New city, no plan, a paper map I've half-circled. Find the one weird museum, the one good bakery, the one bench with the view. Sleep wherever they'll have us. Wake up and do it again.
```

### 1.8 — Family chaos

```
Long brunch with my sisters, all four nieces and nephews running interference. I'm the uncle pretending the chaos isn't on purpose. After they leave, the apartment is loud-quiet, and I take a walk and write down whatever the day made me think.
```

### 1.9 — Studio + porch

```
Whole morning at the studio recording with my band. Lunch nearby — somewhere we always go because the food's good and they don't rush you. Afternoon walking around with no agenda. Evening on a friend's porch, listening to a long story I've already heard.
```

### 1.10 — The minimal version

```
It involves making something — even badly — and sharing food with someone I trust by the end of it. Beyond that I'm flexible. I've learned the perfect day is mostly about who's in it.
```

## PROMPT 2 — "Unpopular opinion…"

10 answer options. These filter hard — pick whichever you'd actually defend in person.

### 2.1 — Music era thesis (your strongest belief)

```
We're living in the greatest era of music ever made. More great records get released in a single month right now than came out in entire decades I won't name. I'll defend this in any nice restaurant of your choosing.
```

### 2.2 — Airports, with the pilot twist

```
Modern air travel is one of the worst experiences of modern life. I say this as someone with a pilot's license. Flying itself is great. Everything around it should be studied as a case in collective consent to misery.
```

### 2.3 — Apartments

```
Modern apartment architecture is going to be looked at the way we look at brutalist parking structures from 1973 — except worse, because at least those had a point of view.
```

### 2.4 — In-N-Out (low-stakes flag-planting)

```
In-N-Out is fine. Just fine. I have tried it many times in good faith. I am not the right west-coaster for that conversation.
```

### 2.5 — Jocks-are-nerds (your own insight)

```
Jocks are basically nerds with muscle and athleticism. The amount of stat memorization, ritualized repetition, and obsessive specialization in a serious athlete is identical to anything in a D&D group. I hold this with affection — I'm both.
```

### 2.6 — Comedy thesis (uses your stated comedic philosophy)

```
The best jokes are not jokes — they're true things you finally said out loud. The bigger the laugh, the more honest the line. Almost all comedy I love operates this way.
```

### 2.7 — Specificity

```
You should have very specific opinions about food, music, and architecture, and very loose opinions about almost everything else. Most people have it backwards.
```

### 2.8 — The excess IS the point (your phrase, lightly polished)

```
Going overboard for someone — a gift, a date, a project — is almost never wrong. The people who tell you it's "too much" are usually the people I'm politely not seeing again.
```

### 2.9 — It's the rooms, not the phones

```
It's not the phones, it's the rooms. We design rooms now where there's nothing to do except look at a phone. Put one good chair in front of a window and the "screen-time problem" mostly takes care of itself.
```

### 2.10 — Non-linear careers

```
Non-linear careers are not "figuring it out." They're a feature. People who did one thing the whole time tend to be missing parts of their brain that make them interesting at 40.
```

## PROMPT 3 — "My favorite topic of conversation is…"

10 answer options. These signal depth and how you talk.

### 3.1 — Shoots and Ladders (your own metaphor)

```
I think about a good conversation the way I think about a good walk: it should go where it goes. Pick any topic and we'll be talking about something else within twenty minutes — that's the whole point.
```

### 3.2 — Stradivarius

```
Anything that connects two things that have no business being connected. Example: Stradivarius violins sound the way they do because of a hundred-year cold spell that produced denser wood. The world is full of this. We're under-talking about it.
```

### 3.3 — History, psychology, the universe

```
History, psychology, and the universe — basically anything where if you keep pulling at the thread, the thread goes another mile. Low patience for small talk, high patience for whatever happens after small talk.
```

### 3.4 — Hot takes you didn't know I had

```
Hot takes you didn't know I had. I have very specific opinions about modern architecture and which sandwiches are wrong, and I don't trade them for free.
```

### 3.5 — Sports + design (your "art guy who watches sports" insight)

```
Why a midrange jumper is a more elegant problem than people think. Why nobody designs lobbies anymore. Why the people who know about both are rare and excellent.
```

### 3.6 — Why something is good (depth marker)

```
Whatever you can't shut up about. I don't actually need to share the topic — I need to watch someone explain why a thing they love is good. The how-they-talk-about-it is the actual data.
```

### 3.7 — Strange facts at 2 AM

```
I collect strange facts — like that the wood in a Stradivarius is, in a real sense, from a volcano — and bring them up at two in the morning. If you're the kind of person who has your own version of this, we'll get along.
```

### 3.8 — Late-night

```
Anything that gets better past midnight. Conversations that can't happen in good lighting. The questions you'd be embarrassed to bring up at brunch.
```

### 3.9 — Why they did it (the moment)

```
Why a person did the specific thing they did. Forget the what, forget the headline — give me the moment they decided. That's almost always the more interesting story.
```

### 3.10 — Truth-jokes connection

```
Anything someone is willing to tell the truth about. The best conversations and the best jokes are basically the same thing — they're the moment someone says the thing nobody usually says.
```

### Why this combination

The four pieces work together — they don't compete:

- **Bio** does the work of *who you are* in compressed form. Uses your most distinctive raw material (career path, family, conversation philosophy).
- **Prompt 1** picks up where the bio leaves off — paints a *scene from your actual life*. The "bed by 11 because I'm not 25" line is the kind of self-aware, age-confident detail that 38–48 readers reward heavily.
- **Prompt 2** plants a flag. Music is your most-defended opinion. It filters out women who aren't going to be your kind of conversation, and lights up women who are. "I'll defend this in any nice restaurant of your choosing" is the closer.
- **Prompt 3** delivers the depth signal. The "good conversation is like a good walk" line is the most quotable thing in your whole profile. It implies the kind of partner you'd actually want — someone whose talk goes somewhere.

The four together imply: maker, family-close, musical, opinionated, conversational, secure, slow-on-pressure but knows what he wants. That's the full picture — and none of it required a single "I'm" sentence.

# Implementation notes

- Paste bio as-is into FB Dating's bio field. If FB shows you a character counter, target 480–500.
- For prompts: pick 3 you'd actually defend out loud. Don't pick the "most clever-sounding" — pick the most *true*.
- Don't change the bio for at least 3 weeks once live. FB Dating's predicted-engagement scoring re-calibrates on every edit; thrash hurts you.
- A/B test prompts, not the bio. Swap out one prompt at a time after 2 weeks if it's underperforming.
